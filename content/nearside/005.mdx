At 2:13 a.m., Elliot Vaughn pressed the call button because the IV pump beside
his bed had started chirping again.

The door stayed closed. A camera above the television woke, and a woman appeared
on the screen with his medication schedule open beside her. The room was in
Ohio. The daylight behind her was not.

## 01 / THE NIGHT NURSE IS ALREADY MORNING

> **SCENE // FICTIONAL**

“I’m Celina Ramos, one of the virtual nurses,” she said. “I can see the pump. I
can’t touch it. I’m calling Ana now.”

Elliot looked at the dark window. “Where are you?”

“Manila. It’s two in the afternoon.”

Celina asked him to confirm his name and date of birth. She watched the pump
through the room camera and entered notes while Ana Brooks, the bedside nurse
covering eight rooms, came through the door and cleared a kink in the line.

The alarm stopped.

Celina handled the screen work and the chart. Ana handled the tube, the patient
and the eight doors that could open at once. Hospitals can split a single act of
care this way because observation and paperwork travel well. Hands still have
to arrive from down the hall.

When Ana returned to the station, three rooms were waiting. Celina had already
prepared notes for two of them. She was one workday ahead by virtue of the
clock, and, according to the reporting behind the system, she may have been
earning a fraction of the U.S. nurse’s hourly pay.

The camera went dark. In Manila, Elliot’s name stayed open until Celina closed
the encounter.

**U.S. hospitals use Filipino virtual healthcare workers**

Rest of World documented Filipino workers performing remote ICU observation and
administrative healthcare tasks for U.S. providers. A small 2026 Mount Sinai
qualitative pilot reported perceived reductions in administrative burden and
overtime, along with communication, training, privacy and integration problems.
Rest of World reported contractor pay of $5 to $10 per hour in the Philippines
and average U.S. registered-nurse pay above $45 per hour. Those figures describe
the workers in its reporting, not a universal pay scale.

## 02 / THE CITY BOOTED FROM A GROUP CHAT

> **CASE // DOCUMENTED**

The ground was still moving when Venezuelan developers began putting pieces of
the country back online.

One site tracked missing people. Others tracked aid, hospital capacity, shelter
and damage. Some of the builders lived outside Venezuela. Family ties and group
chats supplied the requests. Spreadsheets supplied the raw material. General AI
coding tools helped turn both into working public pages.

Developers told Rest of World that jobs which once took about a day sometimes
took a few hours. That is their estimate. The more important fact is visible in
the live directory: volunteers shipped several pieces of emergency
infrastructure while the emergency was still underway.

There was no single control room. Each service knew one part of the disaster,
and each took custody of one part of a person: name, location, photograph,
medical need, sometimes biometric information. Speed solved the first problem
and postponed the second. Someone still had to decide who could search the
records, how long they would remain and what happened after the last missing
person came home.

Cyberpunk once dressed capable outsiders like outlaws. These builders had
ordinary developer accounts and relatives they could not find.

The sites came online before their rules did.

<NearsideArtifact key="venezuela-citizen-ai-earthquake-response" status="REAL NEWS HEADLINE // DOCUMENTED DEPLOYMENT">

**Citizen-built AI disaster infrastructure after Venezuela earthquakes**

Rest of World reported that volunteer teams launched several disaster-response
services after the June 2026 earthquakes. The live project directory documents
platforms for missing people, aid, hospitals, shelters and damage reporting.
The developers supplied the reported build-time improvements. No common public
governance or data-retention framework was identified across the services.

## 03 / NO NEW CAMERA REQUIRED

> **SCENE // FICTIONAL**

Maya Chen kept a paper folder for clients who did not want case documents in a
shared cloud drive. At 9:40 on Friday morning, Luis Ortega placed three more
pages inside it: a corrected address, a vehicle registration and a printout
from the county assessor.

“None of this is secret,” Luis said.

“That’s the problem with public records,” Maya said. “They travel.”

Her monitor showed a report about a planned five-year, $125 million arrangement
between U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement and Thomson Reuters Special
Services. The procurement language quoted in the report covered voter fraud,
immigration fraud and national-security work. It also described continuous
monitoring involving millions of people and entities of interest.

Maya explained CLEAR in terms Luis could use. An address office knows where you
moved. A utility knows when the lights came on. A vehicle file knows what you
drive. A property record knows what belongs to you. Commercial investigation
software can make those separate records answer from one desk.

“Am I in it?” he asked.

“I can’t tell you that.”

The planned contract did not prove that Luis was monitored, listed or accused
of anything. The linked procurement record was blocked in some browsers, and
Thomson Reuters publishes restrictions on using CLEAR solely to locate
noncriminal immigrants for deportation based only on immigration status. The
limits belonged in the record. So did the scale.

Luis slid the county page back into the folder. Nothing new had been installed
on his block. The upgrade lived behind a login he would never see.

**ICE plans a $125 million Thomson Reuters data contract**

## 04 / THE EMPTY SLOT IN THE FIRMWARE

> **SCENE // FICTIONAL**

Nora Bell ran the fabrication room in a public library outside Rochester. The
printers made replacement knobs, science-fair boxes and a surprising number of
small plastic dragons.

On Monday she taped New York Senate Bill S9005C beside the filament cabinet. A
patron named Dev Malik read it while his printer warmed.

“So the machine checks every file now?”

“The governor signed a law,” Nora said. “The machine hasn’t read it.”

The law created a path for future printers to inspect a design and refuse one
classified as a firearm or illegal firearm part. A working group still had to
recommend the standards. If the group found the idea technically infeasible,
the final law did not require implementing rules.

Dev’s file was a replacement latch for a camping stove. The slicer, the program
that turns a 3D model into the printer’s physical moves, handled it exactly as
it had the week before. No classifier checked the shape. No remote switch called
Albany.

The law was already on Nora’s wall. Questions about where a future check would
run, who would update its list, what harmless parts it might reject and whether
design files would leave the room were still waiting for engineers and
regulators.

The latch finished in eleven minutes. Nora peeled it from the build plate and
handed it to Dev.

Behind her, the update screen offered three choices: install, remind me later,
release notes. The refusal command was still an empty slot.

<NearsideArtifact key="new-york-3d-printer-blocking-technology-law" status="REAL NEWS HEADLINE // SIGNED LAW / RULEMAKING PENDING">

**New York requires blocking technology in 3D printers**

New York’s final S9005C text was signed on May 27, 2026. It defines technology
capable of evaluating design files and blocking prints classified as firearms
or illegal firearm parts. A working group must recommend standards.
Implementation remains pending, and regulations are not required if the group
finds the mandate infeasible.

## 05 / THE WALLS ARE TRANSPARENT AT FIVE GIGAHERTZ

> **FIELD EXERCISE // FICTIONAL**

The tenants above Mercado Azul blamed the restaurant’s register for killing
their Wi-Fi every evening. The restaurant blamed the tenants. The internet
provider blamed the building and closed the ticket.

Rafi Soto arrived with a black prototype, a phone and Elena Ruiz’s written
permission to examine the restaurant’s network. The device had four radio
channels listening to the same slice of spectrum at the same time. By comparing
when a signal reached each antenna, it could estimate the direction it came
from. Its campaign called it QuadRF.

Rafi held up the phone. The back room appeared on its camera: shelves, freezer,
ceiling fan. Colored marks floated over the image like compass needles.

“It can see radio?” Elena asked.

“It can point,” he said. “Seeing is the campaign language.”

The strongest burst came from an old wireless bridge zip-tied above the walk-in
freezer. Its power supply warmed, the bridge rebooted, and the upstairs calls
fell apart. Rafi could show direction. He could not promise laboratory accuracy,
name every transmitter or turn a crowdfunding demo into independent proof.

They unplugged the bridge. Three ceilings above them, video calls steadied.

Elena took the phone and turned in a slow circle. Router, payment terminals,
security cameras and customer phones each announced a direction through the
walls. The repair had taken thirty seconds.

She kept looking at the map.

<NearsideArtifact key="quadrf-spatial-radio-camera" status="REAL WORLD TECH // CROWDFUNDING PROTOTYPE">

**QuadRF spatial radio camera**

QuadRF is a crowdfunding prototype for a four-channel coherent
software-defined radio with an integrated Raspberry Pi 5 and coverage from 4.9
to 6.0 GHz. It had not shipped when checked on July 18, 2026. Its creators claim
spatial visualization at 30 frames per second, and campaign tiers ranged from
$499 to $594. The software is published under open-source licenses. The
production RF core, factory DSP bitstreams and key manufacturing files remain
proprietary. Performance evidence is primarily creator-supplied.
