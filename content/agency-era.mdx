Modern Western society was built on the assumption that human intelligence is uniquely valuable. AI is now breaking that assumption. When a foundational premise of an economic system breaks, everything built on top of it reprices. Labor markets will restructure as intelligence becomes abundant and cheap. Capital assets, from equities to real estate to energy, will revalue. And over the next decade, political power will increasingly turn on how governments respond.

## The Cascade of Change

The cascade of change will work like this. AI will commoditize cognitive labor first. The tasks that white-collar professionals get paid to do — analysis, synthesis, writing, code, research, planning — all of it will get significantly cheaper. This is something that's already happening. Companies are quietly doing more with fewer people and the gap between what AI can do and what most knowledge workers do is closing fast.

Digital labor costs will collapse first, but physical labor will follow not long after. As AI gets embedded into robotics, logistics, manufacturing, and infrastructure, the cost of physical work will drop too. It's not going to happen overnight, and it'll probably take many years and cause significant societal tension along the way, but the trajectory is clear and capital markets are already pricing it in.

## The New Bottlenecks: Energy and Compute

As labor costs begin to collapse, that capital will go somewhere else. In this case, the bottleneck shifts from intelligence (we need more people to get the job done) to energy and compute. You can't replace human labor and run the AI economy without massive amounts of power, chips, and physical infrastructure. So capital floods into the things that were supposed to be boring with slow methodical growth — energy production, grid buildout, semiconductors, data centers, raw materials. The physical world becomes a new, exciting frontier.

As AI capabilities expand, new frontiers will begin to open up in places like space, ocean, geothermal, extreme latitudes and much more. The opportunity spaces that were too expensive when human labor was the constraint become viable when that constraint disappears.

## The Breakdown of the Social Contract

Here's the part most people aren't thinking about.

Modern consumer culture, governance models, education systems, even our sense of personal identity and meaning were all built around the idea that you trade your intelligence and time for money and status. That transaction is the foundation of the middle class, the knowledge economy, the entire structure of professional ambition. When intelligence becomes abundant and cheap, that transaction and social contract breaks. The downstream effects on culture, politics, and individual purpose will be as significant as the economic ones. That's why you're already seeing protests against data centers and politicians grandstanding about AI and jobs.

So what do you do about it? The old world demanded labor and rewarded compliance. Show up, do the work, follow the path, collect the paycheck. That's starting to break down. The new world demands nothing and rewards agency. The people who will thrive in this emerging economy are the ones who can identify leverage points, direct AI as a tool, allocate capital and attention toward the right problems, and move without waiting for permission or a playbook. The skill that matters most isn't technical. It's the ability to decide what's worth doing and then do it.

## The Agency Delta

> **The Old World rewarded compliance.** Success was found by following the playbook and providing reliable labor within a known system.

> **The New World rewards agency.** Success is found by identifying leverage, directing AI as a tool, and having the judgment to decide what is worth doing.

## Navigating the Repricing

For capital allocators, that means follow the bottlenecks. Energy, compute, and physical infrastructure are where the constraints live now. For operators and professionals, the actions to be taken are the same but personal. Identify the bottlenecks of AI and build skills that compound on top of AI rather than compete with it. Bottlenecks will manifest as taste, judgment, relationships, ownership of unique and proprietary datasets, the ability to navigate ambiguity, and importantly, assuming accountability and liability for AI. These are the things that get more valuable as intelligence gets cheaper.

This is not a decade-away problem. The repricing is underway right now. The companies restructuring their headcount, the capital flowing into energy and data center buildouts, the political anxiety around AI and jobs — these aren't early signals. They're the main event in its opening act. The people who see it clearly now have a window but it won't stay open long.
