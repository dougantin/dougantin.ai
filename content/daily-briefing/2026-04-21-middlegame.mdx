Today’s memo looks genuinely fresh rather than a stale replay of yesterday’s. The overlap is thematic — AI infrastructure still means chips, power, and grids — but the center of gravity has shifted. Yesterday’s story was about synchronized capital deployment across the stack. Today’s story is narrower and more urgent: the bottlenecks themselves are hardening, and the players who control physical capacity are gaining leverage.

## Foundry capacity is being repriced as strategic scarcity

The clearest signal comes from [TSMC, which reported record Q1 net profit of $18.1 billion, up 58% year over year](https://japantoday.com/category/tech/taiwan's-chipmaker-tsmc-reports-58-jump-in-profit-warns-about-iran-war-impacts), while guiding to even heavier spending ahead. The important point is not just that demand is strong. It is that TSMC now expects the next three years of capex to run materially above the last three, with 2026 spending aimed at the upper end of a $52-56 billion range. That is what a real capacity crunch looks like: demand is no longer merely lifting revenues, it is forcing a global expansion campaign.

That makes [Musk’s Terafab procurement push](https://en.sedaily.com/international/2026/04/16/musks-terafab-seeks-chip-equipment-from-samsung-major) easier to interpret. A project targeting one terawatt of annual computing capacity would have sounded absurd not long ago. Now it reads like an aggressive attempt to secure future chip supply in a world where relying on incumbent foundries may be too expensive, too slow, or too strategically risky.

Even the regional buildout is following the same pattern. [Digital Realty’s plan to invest nearly SG$7 billion in Singapore](https://www.frontier-enterprise.com/digital-realty-eyes-s7-b-investment-to-bolster-singapore-as-apac-ai-hub/) suggests the race is no longer just about who has the best models. It is about who can aggregate enough land, power, interconnection, and financing to turn AI demand into durable infrastructure advantage.

## Power is moving from background condition to active constraint

If foundries are the first bottleneck, power is becoming the second. [Constellation Energy’s plan to add roughly 9,300 MW of capacity through 2030, alongside completed upgrades at Calvert Cliffs](https://www.insidermonkey.com/blog/constellation-energy-ceg-asserts-dominance-on-clean-energy-with-calvert-cliffs-clean-energy-upgrades-1740049/), is a reminder that nuclear operators are being pulled into the AI story because they already own scarce, dispatchable generation.

The market is still willing to finance speculative upside around that theme. [NuScale’s rally continued even as Fluor sold 12.9 million shares for $150.4 million](https://www.dailypolitical.com/2026/04/17/nuscale-power-nysesmr-director-corp-fluor-sells-12936472-shares.html), which says something important about investor psychology: nuclear is no longer being valued purely on near-term fundamentals, but on its option value inside an AI-driven power shortage.

The practical takeaway is that energy is no longer just an enabling input. It is part of the competitive stack. Whoever can secure reliable generation will be in a better position to capture the downstream economics of compute.

## Grid and support infrastructure are the quieter compounding trade

Once compute and generation tighten, the rest of the electrical system starts to matter more. [The HVDC earth electrode line market is projected to grow from $1.61 billion in 2025 to $2.69 billion by 2030](https://www.globenewswire.com/fr/news-release/2026/04/16/3275241/28124/en/High-Voltage-Direct-Current-HVDC-Earth-Electrode-Line-Market-Report-2026-Renewable-Energy-Integration-Fueling-HVDC-Market-Expansion-Worldwide.html), which is another way of saying that long-distance transmission and renewable integration are becoming harder requirements, not optional upgrades.

At the network edge, [5G densification is projected to expand from $18.51 billion in 2025 to $43.21 billion by 2030](https://www.globenewswire.com/fr/news-release/2026/04/17/3276317/28124/en/5G-Network-Densification-Market-Analysis-Report-2026-43-21-Bn-Opportunities-Trends-Competitive-Landscape-Strategies-and-Forecasts-2020-2025-2025-2030F-2035F.html), reinforcing the broader point that the physical communications layer is still being rebuilt for higher-intensity workloads.

And at the facility level, [data-center support infrastructure is expected to grow from $59.22 billion in 2025 to $107.25 billion by 2032](https://www.globenewswire.com/fr/news-release/2026/04/17/3276299/28124/en/107-25-Bn-Data-Center-Support-Infrastructure-Global-Market-Forecasts-2026-2032-Advancements-in-Power-Cooling-AI-enabled-Monitoring-and-Security-Technologies-Redefining-the-Landscap.html). Cooling, monitoring, and power-distribution equipment rarely get the narrative spotlight, but they are exactly where margins and failure risk can concentrate when data-center density rises.

## Middlegame view

The right way to read today’s memo is that AI infrastructure is graduating from a growth story into a capacity-allocation story. [TSMC’s profit surge and spending plans](https://japantoday.com/category/tech/taiwan's-chipmaker-tsmc-reports-58-jump-in-profit-warns-about-iran-war-impacts), [Terafab’s equipment scramble](https://en.sedaily.com/international/2026/04/16/musks-terafab-seeks-chip-equipment-from-samsung-major), and [Constellation’s nuclear expansion posture](https://www.insidermonkey.com/blog/constellation-energy-ceg-asserts-dominance-on-clean-energy-with-calvert-cliffs-clean-energy-upgrades-1740049/) all point in the same direction: physical bottlenecks are becoming the decisive layer of the AI economy.

That means the best lens is no longer “which company has the smartest model?” It is “which company controls scarce industrial capacity?” In this phase of the cycle, foundries, generation, transmission, and data-center support systems look less like background infrastructure and more like the real strategic assets.
