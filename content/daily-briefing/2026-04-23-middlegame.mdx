AI infrastructure is no longer just a software scaling story. Today’s signal is that control over power, fuel, chips, and network infrastructure is becoming the decisive competitive edge—and the constraints are getting more tangible by the week.

## The real bottleneck is physical

The most important shift in today’s memo is that multiple supply chains are tightening at once.

Kazakhstan’s move to build strategic uranium reserves sharpens an already constrained nuclear fuel picture. That matters because uranium is not just a commodity trade here; it is turning into a strategic input for the AI buildout as utilities, reactor developers, and governments all compete for the same future supply. The backdrop is a projected 211 million pound cumulative deficit from 2025 to 2045, with spot uranium near $86/lb and Goldman Sachs targeting $91 by end-2026. [UEC developments](https://www.zerohedge.com/energy/uranium-supply-crunch-worsers-amid-kazakhstans-plan-strategic-reserve)

At the same time, gas-fired backup and bridge capacity looks scarce too. GE Vernova is reportedly sold out on data-center-relevant turbine supply in the near term, which means even buyers with capital are increasingly buying a place in line rather than immediate hardware. [GEV supply constraints](https://www.insidermonkey.com/blog/jim-cramer-notes-that-ge-vernovas-turbine-engines-have-been-in-high-demand-for-data-centers-1741318/)

The implication is straightforward: the AI infrastructure race is being governed less by model ambition and more by who can lock up energy inputs early.

## Nuclear keeps moving from narrative to policy

Washington is still pushing on nuclear from multiple directions. The proposed NEIDA framework would expand DOE authority to license commercial reactors on federal land without NRC approval, creating a more direct path from policy support to deployment. [Federal licensing reform](https://www.zerohedge.com/energy/senate-bill-wants-commercial-reactors-federal-land)

Meanwhile, the White House-backed push into space nuclear is not just speculative theater. It is already catalyzing equity markets and reinforcing the idea that nuclear capability is becoming part of a broader strategic technology stack, from terrestrial grids to lunar infrastructure. [OKLO space program](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/heres-why-oklo-and-other-nuclear-power-stocks-skyrocketed-this-week-151034881.html)

What matters here is less the promotional timeline than the direction of travel: regulatory, defense-adjacent, and hyperscale infrastructure priorities are beginning to align around nuclear capacity.

## Hyperscalers are diversifying the silicon stack

Google’s reported discussions with Marvell mark an important escalation in custom silicon strategy. Instead of relying on a single key partner, Google appears to be broadening its ASIC ecosystem with a memory-processing design and an inference-oriented TPU alongside its long-running Broadcom relationship. [MRVL partnership](https://wccftech.com/google-pulls-marvell-into-a-two-chip-tpu-plan-reshaping-ai-inference-for-asics/)

This is what supply-chain realism looks like at hyperscaler scale: not just better chips, but more optionality around who can build them, package them, and tune them for different workloads.

Tesla’s hiring push in Taiwan points to a similar instinct from a very different company. Recruiting sub-7nm and advanced-packaging talent for Terafab suggests Tesla wants deeper control over compute manufacturing for both robotics and data center ambitions. [Tesla Terafab](https://www.webpronews.com/teslas-taiwan-talent-hunt-signals-bold-leap-into-ai-chip-manufacturing/)

The common theme is vertical integration under supply pressure.

## OpenAI’s retreat is the clearest warning sign

Against that backdrop, OpenAI’s Stargate troubles stand out. Executive departures, cancelled European expansion plans, and a shift away from owned infrastructure toward rented capacity all suggest that building a sovereign compute empire is harder, slower, and more capital-intensive than the market narrative implied. [Stargate setbacks](https://www.webpronews.com/openais-stargate-gambit-hits-snags-as-amazon-steps-in-with-50-billion-bet/)

That does not mean demand is fading. It means the balance of advantage may be shifting toward companies that already control land, power, supply agreements, packaging relationships, and utility-scale partners.

## Sovereignty is spreading from data to the grid

India’s draft National Electricity Policy adds another layer to the story: infrastructure nationalism is moving beyond semiconductors and cloud into energy data itself. The policy would require power-sector operational data to remain in-country while still mandating sharing for AI use cases. [Grid data requirements](https://www.thehindubusinessline.com/business-tech/when-the-grid-becomes-an-all-knowing-data-system/article70881361.ece)

Equinix’s Fabric Intelligence launch fits neatly on the other side of that trend. If AI agents can compress network deployment from weeks to minutes, then the control plane around infrastructure becomes more software-defined—but also more strategic and more central to operators that already own the physical footprint. [EQIX platform](https://completeaitraining.com/news/equinix-launches-fabric-intelligence-to-automate-network/)

## Bottom line

Today’s memo looks fresh, and its message is sharper than the usual “AI capex keeps rising” refrain. The defining constraint is now industrial: fuel, turbines, licensing, fabs, packaging, and sovereign control over infrastructure data. In that world, the winners are not merely the best model builders. They are the players that can secure scarce physical inputs before everyone else realizes those inputs are the real market.
