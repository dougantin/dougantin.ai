The AI buildout is entering a new phase: less wonder, more concrete. The headline is not a flashy model release but the sheer industrial scale behind the next leg of the market. Hyperscalers are preparing to spend an estimated $660–690 billion in 2026 alone on capital expenditures, part of a multi-year infrastructure push that is beginning to look less like a tech cycle and more like a wartime mobilization of power, steel, cooling, and land.

That shift matters because it clarifies where the real action is. AI’s next bottlenecks are no longer primarily chips or algorithms. They are electricity, permits, transmission, and the ability to build physical systems faster than demand compounds.

## The market is voting for power first

If there is one throughline across today’s memo, it is that energy has moved from supporting actor to lead character. Bank of America’s framing of nuclear as a potential $10 trillion opportunity is a sign of just how mainstream the thesis has become. For years, nuclear exposure in public markets was treated as a speculative side bet. Now it is increasingly being discussed as a strategic necessity for data center operators facing extreme and persistent load growth.

That does not mean every nuclear-adjacent name is investable. Oklo and NuScale may be well positioned on paper, but early-stage nuclear companies still face the familiar problems of regulation, execution, cost overruns, and time. The market’s willingness to entertain the category is real; the market’s ability to distinguish narrative from deliverable megawatts will matter even more.

Meanwhile, incumbent power operators are already monetizing the surge. NRG’s expanded data center power agreements and multi-gigawatt pipeline are the more grounded signal here. The companies that can contract, generate, and route electricity at scale are moving closer to the center of the AI stack.

## AI infrastructure is becoming politically contested

The second big development is more subtle, but arguably more important over the medium term: communities are beginning to push back. Resistance to data center projects in places like Archbald, Pennsylvania, is a reminder that AI infrastructure does not arrive in a vacuum. It arrives with transmission lines, water use, construction noise, land-use disputes, and questions about who absorbs higher electricity bills.

This is the kind of friction the market tends to underprice early. Wall Street can model capex plans and GPU demand. It is much worse at modeling local politics. But permitting fights and public resistance can slow projects just as effectively as supply shortages, especially when proposed campuses reach industrial scale.

The result is a more complicated picture than the simple "AI equals more data centers" trade. Demand is obvious. Deployment is not. Investors should expect a widening gap between announced infrastructure and actually energized infrastructure.

## The picks-and-shovels trade still looks healthier than the platform trade

If you want the cleanest beneficiaries of this cycle, the evidence continues to point toward specialized infrastructure vendors rather than the broad AI narrative basket. Vertiv’s continued outperformance and Hubbell’s demand tailwinds reflect a market rewarding companies tied directly to power distribution, thermal management, and electrical equipment.

That makes sense. In a gold rush, the highest-confidence revenue often sits with the firms solving immediate constraints. Cooling systems, switchgear, backup power, and interconnects are not glamorous, but they are non-optional. The more hyperscaler spending concentrates into real-world deployment, the more valuable those suppliers become.

This also helps explain why operational efficiency stories are starting to matter. Globe Telecom’s AI-driven cooling deployment, with reported 20–30% annual energy savings, points to a second-order theme: once power becomes expensive and scarce, optimization technologies stop being nice-to-haves and start becoming procurement priorities.

## Geopolitics remains embedded in the stack

Japan’s additional support for Rapidus is another reminder that AI infrastructure is also industrial policy. Governments are no longer content to treat advanced semiconductor manufacturing as a market outcome. They want domestic footholds in the supply chain, even when incumbents like TSMC retain a commanding lead.

The practical takeaway is straightforward: the AI buildout is making national champions out of whoever can plausibly provide chips, power, or manufacturing resilience. That should keep subsidy regimes, strategic partnerships, and state-backed competition central to the story for years.

## What matters now

Three conclusions stand out.

First, AI infrastructure spending is real, enormous, and still accelerating. The demand signal is not the question anymore.

Second, the limiting factors are migrating from model quality to industrial execution. Power generation, grid readiness, permitting, and community acceptance are becoming gating variables.

Third, the best-positioned winners may not be the loudest AI brands. They may be the utilities, cooling companies, electrical suppliers, and energy developers that convert capex intent into operational capacity.

The market spent the first phase of the AI boom pricing intelligence. It is now pricing the cost of hosting that intelligence in the physical world. That is a more grounded story—and probably a more durable one.
